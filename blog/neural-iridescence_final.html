§ 0 — Précis

An iridescent BRDF, integrated over the sky, then forgotten

Path-traced iridescence is brutally expensive. Because, a thin-film Fresnel oscillates sharply with viewing angle, then it is multiplied by a microfacet specular peak and an environment map, the resulting integrand has frequency content far above what low-discrepancy sampling can resolve at any reasonable budget (i.e. spp bellow 1024). A converged frame needs thousands of BRDF evaluations per pixel. We want the same look at full frame rate.

For this we can observe that under a uniform white sky the converged radiance at any point on a perfect sphere depends only on $\cos\theta_v = \mathbf{n}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}_o$ or one scalar. We can then fit that scalar function with a Fourier-encoded MLP whose width and depth fall out of a continuous architecture search to shrink a MLP so it can run at interactive frame rate.

§ 1 — The integral

What a fragment shader is supposed to compute

Outgoing radiance at a surface point in direction $\boldsymbol{\omega}_o$ is the hemispherical integral

$$L_o(\boldsymbol{\omega}_o)=\int_{\Omega} f(\boldsymbol{\omega}_i,\boldsymbol{\omega}_o)\, L_i(\boldsymbol{\omega}_i)\, (\mathbf{n}\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\omega}_i)\, d\boldsymbol{\omega}_i$$
(1)

Eq. 1 is the rendering equation restricted to a single bounce. The radiance $L_o$ leaving the surface in direction $\boldsymbol{\omega}_o$ is the sum, over every incoming direction $\boldsymbol{\omega}_i$ on the upper hemisphere $\Omega$, of three multiplied terms: the incident radiance $L_i(\boldsymbol{\omega}_i)$ arriving from that direction, the BRDF $f(\boldsymbol{\omega}_i,\boldsymbol{\omega}_o)$ giving the fraction of it redirected toward the camera, and the cosine factor $\mathbf{n}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}_i$ that accounts for foreshortening at grazing angles. Evaluate this integral at every shaded pixel and you have a rendered image.

Although the integration domain is only two-dimensional, this can make the problem appear simpler than it actually is. The integrand depends on three unit vectors: the outgoing direction $\boldsymbol{\omega}_o$, the surface normal $\mathbf{n}$, and the incoming direction $\boldsymbol{\omega}_i$. These vectors also implicitly define the half-vector on which the BRDF depends. Once the iridescent BRDF described in later sections is substituted into the integrand, this dependence becomes expensive enough to challenge real-time rendering budgets.

The output, however, is much lower-dimensional. Under a uniform sky and an isotropic material, symmetry reduces the outgoing radiance $L_o$ to a function of a single scalar: $\cos\theta_v$. This scalar is precisely the input to our compact neural network.

§ 2 — The BRDF

Three lobes, one energy budget

The material response used in the lighting integral is modeled as a sum of three coupled lobes: a rough specular term, a diffuse remainder, and a clearcoat layer.

$$f \;=\; \underbrace{D\cdot V\cdot F_\text{base}}_{\text{rough specular}} \;+\; \underbrace{(1{-}F_\text{base})\cdot \text{B}(\boldsymbol{\omega}_i,\boldsymbol{\omega}_o,\alpha)}_{\text{diffuse remainder}} \;+\; \underbrace{D_\text{cc}\cdot V_\text{cc}\cdot F_\text{cc}}_{\text{clearcoat}}$$

Here, $D$ denotes the GGX normal distribution function, $V$ is the Smith correlated visibility term, and $\text{B}$ is Disney’s roughness-dependent diffuse model, including its retroreflective component. The terms $F_\text{base}$ and $F_\text{cc}$ are spectrally integrated thin-film Fresnel terms evaluated in closed form. We model them as a three-layer dielectric stack: air, a thin film of thickness $d_\text{inc}$ and refractive index $\eta_2$, and a substrate with refractive index $\eta_3$. The evaluation assumes unpolarized light and truncates the internal reflection series at $m \le 3$ bounces.

The three lobes are not independent. Two coupling terms combine them into a single energy budget. First, the diffuse term is scaled by $(1-F_\text{base})$, so energy already reflected by the base specular layer is not also scattered diffusely. This is a Schlick-style energy partition, and it is also the mechanism through which the iridescent color influences the diffuse channel. Second, the combined base-specular and diffuse response is attenuated by $\max(0, 1-F_\text{cc})$, allowing the clearcoat to act as a partial mirror layered above the rest of the material.

The following material constants are fixed during training: albedo = (1,1,1), roughness = 0.45, metalness = 0.90, clearcoat = 0.95, η₂ = 1.40, η₃ = 1.05, and d_inc = 1.20. Changing any of these constants changes the target function, so the network must be retrained.

§ 3 — Thin-film Fresnel

Where the colour comes from

The iridescent appearance is not caused by pigment. It comes from optical interference. A thin film of thickness $d_\text{inc}$ and refractive index $\eta_2$ lies on top of a substrate with refractive index $\eta_3$. When light reaches the film, part of it is reflected at the air-film interface, while the rest is transmitted into the film, reflects from the film-substrate interface, and exits back into the air. The transmitted component therefore travels an additional optical path length:

$$\Delta = 2\,\eta_2\,d_\text{inc}\,\cos\theta_t$$

This path difference depends on the refracted angle $\theta_t$. When the reflected wavefronts recombine, they interfere with one another. Wavelengths satisfying $\Delta = m\lambda$ are reinforced, while wavelengths satisfying $\Delta = (m{+}1/2)\lambda$ are attenuated. As the view angle changes from grazing to normal incidence, the optical path difference changes as well, causing the visible colour bands to move through the spectrum.

Belcour and Barla approximate the spectral integration by fitting the CIE 1931 colour matching functions as sums of Gaussians. This makes the product of the oscillatory interference term and each Gaussian analytically integrable. The result is a closed-form expression for the spectrally integrated Fresnel term, evaluated per RGB channel and summed over a small number of internal-reflection orders, here $m = 0,1,2,3$. Higher-order terms decay geometrically and are neglected.

The visualization below evaluates this closed-form model using a simplified two-beam approximation. Although this is not the full Belcour series, it has the same qualitative angular structure: reflectance varies with $\cos\theta_v$, and each RGB channel follows a different interference pattern. Adjusting $d_\text{inc}$ shifts the band structure, while changing $\eta_2$ affects both the phase and the amplitude of the response. The network used in this work is trained only for the specific material parameters shown here.

§ 4 — Building targets

Three importance-sampling strategies, balanced

Once the Fresnel term includes thin-film interference, the lighting integral in Eq. 1 no longer has a practical closed-form solution. We therefore build the supervision targets with Monte Carlo integration. Naive uniform sampling is too noisy for this problem. m Most samples miss the narrow specular features, especially the clearcoat peak. This is the same variance problem that makes the BRDF expensive to render directly.

To reduce variance, we use multiple importance sampling with the balance heuristic:

$$\hat L_o = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{s=1}^{N_k} w_k(\boldsymbol{\omega}_{i,s})\, \frac{ f\,L_i\,(\mathbf{n}\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\omega}_i) }{ p_k(\boldsymbol{\omega}_{i,s}) }, \qquad w_k(\boldsymbol{\omega}) = \frac{ N_k\,p_k(\boldsymbol{\omega}) }{ \sum_j N_j\,p_j(\boldsymbol{\omega}) }$$
(2)

We combine three sampling strategies. The first is cosine-weighted hemisphere sampling, which targets the diffuse lobe. The second is VNDF sampling with $\alpha = 0.20$, which targets the base specular lobe. The third is VNDF sampling with $\alpha_\text{cc} = 0.0156$, which targets the near-mirror clearcoat lobe.

Each strategy places samples where one part of the BRDF contributes most. The balance weight $w_k$ then combines the strategies without double-counting energy. This is important because the strategies overlap strongly near the center of the hemisphere: a direction sampled by the diffuse strategy may also have non-negligible probability under the specular strategies. The balance heuristic accounts for this overlap and keeps the estimator unbiased.

For each grid point, we use $N_k = 2048$ samples per strategy, or roughly six thousand BRDF evaluations per supervision target. The full dataset contains ten thousand grid points. In practice, this target-building pass takes a few minutes on a single CUDA device and must be repeated whenever the material parameters change.

§ 5 — The reduction

Radiance as a function of one scalar

This symmetry is what makes the approximation tractable. A sphere is rotationally symmetric, and a uniform white sky is also rotationally symmetric. When the material is isotropic, the converged outgoing radiance at a surface point depends only on the angle between the surface normal and the viewing direction:

$$\cos\theta_v \;=\; \mathbf{n}\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\omega}_o \;\in\; [0,1]$$

Rotating $\boldsymbol{\omega}_o$ around $\mathbf{n}$ does not change the lighting, because $L_i$ is constant. It also does not change the BRDF, because an isotropic material has no preferred tangent direction. The integral therefore evaluates to the same value for every outgoing direction with the same $\mathbf{n}\!\cdot\!\boldsymbol{\omega}_o$.

In other words, although the integrand is written in terms of several three-dimensional directions, the final radiance is not a high-dimensional function under these assumptions. After marginalizing over incident directions, the result can be represented as a scalar function of $\cos\theta_v$.

We exploit this directly: instead of evaluating the full lighting integral at runtime, we fit a compact scalar function over $\cos\theta_v$. The network is not learning an arbitrary angular BRDF. It is learning the already-integrated radiance profile induced by this specific material and lighting setup.

The terminal-ray approximation

At render time, this gives a simple approximation strategy. When the path tracer hits the iridescent surface, we evaluate the network and terminate the bounce loop. No secondary ray is traced from that hit point.

For an isolated sphere under a constant environment, with no occlusion or interreflection, this matches the preintegrated target exactly. In a more general scene, it becomes an approximation: the sphere no longer sees only a uniform sky, and surrounding geometry can alter the incident radiance. In practice, it remains useful when the iridescent object is the dominant reflective element and the rest of the scene is mostly diffuse. The shader is reduced to a primary-ray hit, a scalar lookup or network evaluation, and no recursive material integration.

§ 6 — Spectral bias and Fourier features

Why a vanilla MLP cannot fit iridescence.

A plain ReLU MLP, trained on a target with rich frequency content, fits low-frequency components first; the gap to high-frequency content grows exponentially in frequency (Rahaman et al. 2019). For the iridescent target this means the diffuse trend is fit perfectly, the base specular falloff approximately, and the iridescent banding not at all.

The fix (Mildenhall et al. 2020 / Tancik et al. 2020) is to lift $\cos\theta_v$ through a deterministic positional encoding before the MLP:

$$\gamma(x) = \big[\,x,\; \sin(\pi x), \cos(\pi x), \sin(2\pi x), \cos(2\pi x),\;\ldots,\; \sin(2^{L-1}\pi x), \cos(2^{L-1}\pi x)\,\big]$$

The encoded dimension is $1 + 2L$. Tancik's NTK analysis says training a ReLU MLP on $\gamma(x)$ is equivalent to kernel regression with a kernel whose effective bandwidth is set by the highest frequency in $\gamma$. The MLP itself is no less smooth-biased; what changed is that the coordinate system now contains the high-frequency basis explicitly.

The raw $x$ channel is kept alongside the Fourier features so that slowly-varying envelopes — diffuse, clearcoat trends — do not have to be reconstructed from a sum of bandlimited pieces.

L's roleThe maximum frequency in $\gamma$ is $2^{L-1}\pi$. Doubling $L$ doubles bandwidth at additive cost in input dimension (and a small additive cost in fc0 width). Cheaper than scaling depth or width to reach the same kernel.

§ 7 — The network

Two ReLU layers, three matrix multiplies.

Encoded input fed through a depth-2 MLP. Three multiplies, two ReLUs, one non-negative clamp:

$$\begin{aligned} h_0 &= \mathrm{ReLU}(W_0\,\gamma(x) + b_0),\quad W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{H\times(1+2L)} \\ h_1 &= \mathrm{ReLU}(W_1\, h_0 + b_1),\quad W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{H\times H} \\ f_\theta(x) &= \max\!\big(0,\; W_2\, h_1 + b_2\big),\quad W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times H} \end{aligned}$$

Output is clamped to $\ge 0$ — radiance is non-negative — but not to $\le 1$. The integrated radiance is HDR by design (peaks above 1 at grazing angles); the shader's downstream Reinhard tonemap is what compresses it for display. Clamping the network to $[0,1]$ would crush the highlights before they reached the tonemap.

Why two layersDepth-1 cannot represent the local minima between iridescent bands without absurd width. Depth-3 fits as well as depth-2 here but doubles inference cost. Two is the smallest that suffices.

§ 8 — How (L, H) chose itself

A DARTS-style continuous relaxation, narrowed to two scalars.

We do not pick $L$ and $H$ by hand. They fall out of a continuous relaxation (Liu et al. 2019) specialised down to two scalar knobs.

Build a supernet at maximum capacity ($L_\text{max}=6$, $H_\text{max}=16$, ≈ 600 parameters) and add two vectors of learnable sigmoid gates: $\boldsymbol{\alpha}\in\mathbb{R}^{L_\text{max}}$, one gate per Fourier octave, multiplying its sin/cos pair; and $\boldsymbol{\beta}\in\mathbb{R}^{H_\text{max}}$, one gate per hidden unit, multiplying its post-ReLU activation. The same $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ multiplies both hidden layers, so the layer widths shrink in lock-step (one integer $H$ to ship as a #define). Gates initialise to logit $3$ — $\sigma(3)\approx 0.953$ — so the supernet starts almost fully on; training begins indistinguishable from the dense baseline.

The raw $x$ channel is never gated. Dropping it would force the network to reconstruct a constant from a sum of sines.

The loss

MSE plus a differentiable parameter-count proxy:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta,\boldsymbol{\alpha},\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \mathbb{E}_x\,\big\|f_\theta^g(x)-\hat L_o^\text{shader}(x)\big\|^2 \;+\; \lambda\,\frac{\text{proxy}(\boldsymbol{\alpha},\boldsymbol{\beta})}{\text{proxy}_\text{max}}$$
$$\text{proxy} = \big(2 + 2\textstyle\sum_k \sigma(\alpha_k)\big)\bar H + \bar H^2 + \bar H + 3\bar H + 3,\qquad \bar H=\textstyle\sum_j \sigma(\beta_j)$$

The proxy is the closed-form parameter count of the MLP with $L$ and $H$ replaced by their gate-weighted continuous analogues. It is differentiable, recovers the integer count exactly when every gate is binary, and its gradient with respect to each $\beta$ grows linearly in $\bar H$ — so the $n$-th unit costs more than the first. Both pulls go toward zero; only gates whose absence would meaningfully raise MSE resist.

The λ ramp

Setting $\lambda=10^{-3}$ from epoch zero collapses everything before the MLP has learned anything to defend. Instead $\lambda$ is held at zero for the first 20% of training, then linearly ramped to $10^{-3}$ by the 50% mark, then held. First 20% — clean fit at full capacity. Next 30% — increasing pressure to shed weight. Final 50% — consolidation under steady pressure.

After training, threshold each gate at 0.5: keep above, drop below. Result on this problem: $L^*=2$, $H^*=7$, 122 parameters. Twenty-two gates shed.

Why retrain

Critically, the gated supernet at the end of search is not equivalent to a clean MLP at $(L^*, H^*)$. Surviving gates are $\sigma(\beta)\approx 0.95$, not exactly 1. The "dropped" weight columns still exist, multiplied by ≈ 0, carrying whatever noise they absorbed during training. So we discard the supernet and retrain a clean ungated MLP at $(L^*, H^*)$ with the same data, optimiser and schedule. Final MSE matches the over-parameterised baseline within noise.

λ sensitivityThe chosen $10^{-3}$ is in the small range where MSE-gradient and proxy-gradient on the gates are comparable in the first epoch after the ramp completes. Outside that range one term wins and the search becomes degenerate — either no shedding, or total collapse.

Same β for both layersOtherwise we ship two integers $H_0, H_1$. With one $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ the network is forced to commit to a single width; the search is more decisive and the runtime smaller.

§ 9 — Inference

Twelve lines of GLSL.

The trained weights are emitted as const float[] blocks beside an encoder and a forward pass. In a fragment shader, the entire iridescent BRDF branch becomes one call to neuralIridescence(dot(N, -V)) and a break. Everything else — ray-sphere intersection, environment lookup, tonemap — is untouched.

// γ : R → R^(1+2L)
void nrEncode(float x, out float feat[NR_IN]) {
    feat[0] = x;
    for (int k = 0; k < NR_L; k++) {
        float a = x * 3.14159265 * exp2(float(k));
        feat[1 + k]        = sin(a);
        feat[1 + NR_L + k] = cos(a);
    }
}

// f_θ : R → R^3, non-negative
vec3 neuralIridescence(float cosThetaV) {
    float feat[NR_IN]; nrEncode(cosThetaV, feat);
    float h0[NR_H];
    for (int i = 0; i < NR_H; i++) {
        float s = NR_B0[i];
        for (int j = 0; j < NR_IN; j++) s += NR_W0[i*NR_IN + j] * feat[j];
        h0[i] = max(0.0, s);
    }
    // ... h1 (NR_H×NR_H), output (3×NR_H), return clamp(out, 0.0, +inf)
}

§ 10 — Where error concentrates

The residual is local, not visible.

Two regions account for nearly all the residual MSE:

  • $\cos\theta_v \approx 0$ (grazing). The iridescent specular peak is at its sharpest. The $L=2$ basis tops out at frequency $4\pi$ and can separate the dominant bands but not fit them with vanishing residual.
  • $\cos\theta_v \approx 0.5$. The $m=2$ multi-bounce term in the iridescent Fresnel crosses zero with a high derivative — a brief sharp colour transition that the network smooths slightly.

Both residuals are $O(10^{-3})$ MSE and not visible on the rendered sphere. Per channel, blue typically converges slightly faster than red and green, because at $d_\text{inc}=1.20$, $\eta_2=1.40$ the blue band lives in a flat region of the CIE-1931 sensitivity, while red and green sit on steeper portions that depend more on the higher-order $C_m$ coefficients.

Could L=3 fix itYes. It also ships an extra octave (4 more weights into fc0) and shaves about $5\times 10^{-4}$ off the MSE in the grazing region. Below the noise floor of the supervision target. Not worth the parameters.

§ 11 — Limits

What is baked in.

The network trades flexibility for size. Four things are constants of training, not inputs:

  • Material. Every coefficient — roughness, metalness, clearcoat, $\eta_2$, $\eta_3$, $d_\text{inc}$, both $\eta_1$, albedo — is baked. Change any and the network must be retrained.
  • Lighting. Trained against a uniform white sky. A different env map, even one with a sun, breaks the rotational-symmetry assumption that justified the 1D input.
  • Scene. Terminal-ray treatment is exact only for an isolated sphere. Adds bounded approximation error in scenes with multiple iridescent objects or strong inter-object reflections.
  • Domain. Trained on $\cos\theta_v \in [0,1]$. Defensive clamp(x, 0, 1) at the call site is enough; the output clamp keeps the result non-negative.

The right way to read those constraints: the radiance net is not a general iridescent material. It is a compiled cache of one specific iridescent material under one specific sky on one specific object. The compiler is the training run.

Why not condition on the BRDF parameters?

The obvious extension is to feed $(\eta_2, d_\text{inc}, \alpha, \dots)$ into the network alongside $\cos\theta_v$, training one general model that responds to material sliders at runtime. I considered it and chose not to. The reason is that iridescence is, in the parameter space, an extraordinarily high-frequency function: nudge $d_\text{inc}$ by a few percent and the colour bands slide across the visible spectrum, swap order, sometimes invert. The 1D slice along $\cos\theta_v$ that this network compresses is already near the limit of what $L=2$ Fourier features and seven hidden units can resolve cleanly; lifting the same surface into a 4- or 5-dimensional input would multiply the required Fourier basis, the supervision grid (10 000 points along one axis becomes millions across several), and the training compute. The resulting network would be tens of kilobytes of weights rather than 122 floats, and the inference cost in MADs would scale with the increased width.

Shadertoy fragment shaders have a tight envelope — uniform storage budget, source-length limits, single-pass compilation, and the social expectation that the whole thing fits in one tab. The 122-parameter, twelve-line network meets that envelope; a conditional one would not. So I baked the parameters and left the door open: when the budget is bigger (a desktop renderer, a game engine), conditioning on $(d_\text{inc}, \eta_2)$ first is the natural step, and the same DARTS pipeline applies — it would just choose larger $L^*$ and $H^*$.

GeneralisingConditioning the network on $(d_\text{inc}, \eta_2)$ would bring iridescent-thickness sliders back at the cost of a wider input and a larger network. We did not. The cost-budget for this project was unconditional throughput, and the Shadertoy envelope.

§ 12 — Related shaders

What this replaces, and what came before.

The network compresses what was originally a Monte Carlo path tracer running entirely in a fragment shader. Both originals are below — published on Shadertoy and embedded live. They render the same iridescent sphere under the same uniform white sky, but evaluate the BRDF analytically with hundreds to thousands of samples per pixel rather than calling a 122-parameter MLP once.
